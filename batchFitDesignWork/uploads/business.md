# BatchFit — Business Plan & Brand Brief

> **Working name:** BatchFit · **Tagline:** *Prep. Track. Get fit.*
> **Document purpose:** Company overview and brand foundation — detailed enough for a designer or agency to produce a logo, brand identity, and website without further briefing.
> **Status:** Pre-build. Personal-use MVP first, with a clear path to a published commercial product.

---

## 1. Executive Summary

BatchFit is a weight-loss and body-composition app built around the way committed people *actually* eat: **batch cooking and meal prep.** Most calorie trackers treat every meal as a one-off lookup. BatchFit treats the **prepped batch** as the core unit — you log a cook once (e.g. 1.2 kg chicken breast, 250 g cornflour, 800 g rice), split it into portions, and the app calculates the macros *per portion* and tracks how many meals you have left in your inventory.

The result is the first tracker that mirrors the real workflow of people who meal-prep to lose weight: **cook in bulk on Sunday, eat from stock all week, log in one tap, and always know what's in the fridge.** Around this differentiated core sits a complete weight-loss toolkit: food logging with full macros (calories, protein, fat, carbs, fibre), workout/strength logging, bodyweight trend tracking, and adaptive calorie targets.

---

## 2. The Problem / Market Need

People serious about losing weight overwhelmingly turn to **meal prepping** — cooking large batches in advance to control portions, hit macro targets, save time, and remove daily decision fatigue. Yet every major tracking app fails this workflow:

- **Repetitive, friction-heavy logging.** Prepping the same meal six times means re-finding and re-entering it six times. Logging is a chore, and friction is the #1 reason people abandon trackers.
- **No concept of a "batch."** Apps log *foods*, not *cooks*. There's no native way to say "I made this in bulk, split it 6 ways — tell me the macros per portion." People resort to spreadsheets and calculators alongside the app.
- **No inventory.** Nothing tracks *how many prepped meals you have left*. Meal-preppers manage this in their heads or on paper.
- **Bloated, dated UX.** Market leaders (MyFitnessPal in particular) are cluttered, ad-heavy, and increasingly paywalled, frustrating a large, motivated user base.

**The gap:** there is no well-designed, prep-first tracker that understands batch cooking as a first-class concept. BatchFit fills it.

### Why now
- Meal prep is a mainstream, durable habit (huge, active communities across social platforms and a steady stream of prep-focused content and creators).
- Sustained backlash against MyFitnessPal's monetisation has primed users to switch.
- Modern phones make the hard part — barcode scanning, on-device data, fast UX — trivial to deliver well.

## 3. Vision, Mission & Goals

**Vision:** Make eating for your goals as simple as cooking once and eating from stock — so that staying on track requires almost no daily effort.

**Mission:** Build the tracker that meal-preppers wish existed: batch cooking and portion macros as the core, wrapped in the fastest, cleanest logging experience on the market.

**Goals**

| Horizon | Goal |

## 4. The Product — Core Concept First

### 4.1 The batch-cooking engine (the heart of the product)

This is the feature everything else is designed around, and the story the brand should tell.

1. **Create a batch / recipe.** Name it ("Chicken & Rice"), add ingredients by weight — each pulls macros per 100 g from the food database (1.2 kg chicken breast, 250 g cornflour, 800 g cooked rice, etc.).
2. **The app totals the whole batch** — calories, protein, fat, carbs, fibre across everything cooked.
3. **Split into portions.** Tell it "6 meals" → it divides totals by 6 to give **macros per portion.**
4. **The batch enters your inventory** — "Chicken & Rice — 6 portions left."
5. **Eat from stock.** One tap logs a portion to today's totals **and decrements the count.** Inventory shows, at a glance, how many prepped meals you have ready and roughly how many days of food you're stocked for.

Recipes are reusable templates; each *cook* snapshots the actual amounts used (real cooking varies batch to batch), so accuracy stays honest.

### 4.2 Surrounding toolkit
- **Food logging** with full macros: calories, protein, fat, carbs, fibre — logged to meals (breakfast/lunch/dinner/snack), with daily totals vs. targets.
- **Strength & workout logging:** custom exercises; sets, reps, weight (plus bodyweight, time, and distance modes); "repeat last workout."
- **Bodyweight tracking** stored raw, displayed as a smoothed trend (daily weight is noisy; the trend is the truth).
- **Targets:** calorie + macro goals, with a TDEE-based calculator and (Phase 3) adaptive targets that adjust weekly from real intake vs. weight change.

## 5. Target Audience

**Primary persona — "The Sunday Prepper"**
- 25–45, wants to lose fat and/or build muscle, treats it seriously but isn't a pro.
- Already meal preps, or wants to start. Cooks in bulk to save time and stay disciplined.
- Currently juggling MyFitnessPal + a notes app + a calculator, and finds it tedious.
- **Wants:** less daily friction, accurate per-portion macros, and to feel organised and in control.

**Secondary personas**
- **The gym-focused lifter** who tracks both training and protein and wants them in one place.
- **The budget-and-time-conscious cook** who preps to save money and reduce waste, and values the inventory/stock view.

**Mindset to design for:** motivated but time-poor; values *control, efficiency, and progress*; wants tools that feel like a competent ally, not a nagging diet app.

## 7. Business Model (Phase 3+)

- **Free tier:** core logging, manual food entry, limited batch recipes — enough to be genuinely useful and build habit.
- **Premium subscription (monthly/annual):** unlimited recipes/batches, barcode scanning, cloud sync across devices, adaptive targets, progress photos, advanced trends/analytics.
- **Principles:** no ads, no selling data. The MyFitnessPal backlash is a feature of the market — BatchFit wins partly by *not* being that. Pricing positioned competitively against MacroFactor/Premium MFP.

*(Personal-use MVP has no monetisation; this section guides brand decisions so the identity scales to a paid product.)*

## 8. Brand Identity Brief (for the designer)

This section is the design-facing brief. Logo and website should express the personality and direction below.

### 8.1 Brand personality
BatchFit is a **capable, no-nonsense ally** — like a well-organised friend who's great in the kitchen and the gym. The five traits:

- **Organised** — order from chaos; the calm of a well-stocked fridge and a clear plan.
- **Efficient** — fast, frictionless, respects your time. One tap, done.
- **Strong / progressive** — about getting fitter and forward momentum, not restriction or guilt.
- **Warm, not clinical** — encouraging and human; food is enjoyed, not feared.
- **Honest** — no fads, no ads, no diet-industry shame. Just clarity.

**We are:** practical, motivating, clean, modern, a bit appetising.
**We are not:** clinical, preachy, "wellness"-fluffy, intimidating gym-bro, or cluttered.

### 8.2 Tone of voice
Plain, direct, encouraging. Short sentences. Speaks like a knowledgeable friend, never a drill sergeant or a diet guru. Light, dry humour is welcome; shame and hype are not.
- ✅ "Six meals, sorted. Here's your macros per portion."
- ✅ "Three prepped meals left — time for a cook-up?"
- ❌ "Crush your goals! Transform your life!!" / ❌ "You've exceeded your limit."

### 8.3 Visual direction
**Logo concepts to explore** (the batch-cooking story is the richest seam):
- A **stockpot / batch pot**, or a meal-prep container, possibly subdivided into portions — visually encoding "one cook, many meals."
- A **grid / portions motif** (a divided container, segmented ring) tying "batch → portions" to the data/tracking side.
- A clean wordmark with a distinctive, friendly mark that works tiny (app icon) and reads instantly.
- Avoid tired fitness clichés: no flexing biceps, no flames, no dumbbell-as-the-"I", no tomato-and-tape-measure diet imagery.

**Colour mood:** fresh, appetising, energetic but clean. Think **fresh greens** (food, health, freshness) as a likely lead, balanced with a confident, modern accent (warm coral/orange for appetite and energy, or a deep slate/charcoal for a premium, focused base). Avoid sickly "diet" pastels and avoid aggressive red-alert tones — this app never shames.

**Imagery & style:** real, appetising prepped food in tidy containers; clean flat-lay/organised aesthetics; generous whitespace; rounded, friendly geometry. UI should feel like a *premium kitchen tool* — uncluttered, tactile, satisfying. Modern, highly legible sans-serif typography (numbers matter — macros and counts must be crisp and scannable).

**Design north star:** *the calm satisfaction of a perfectly organised fridge full of prepped meals.* Every screen and the website should feel that organised, that in-control, that appetising.

### 8.4 Website priorities
The site should, in order:
1. **Lead with the batch-cooking hook** — "Cook once. Eat all week. Macros sorted." Show the batch → portions → inventory flow visually above the fold; this is the differentiator and the reason to care.
2. Show the **per-portion macro calculation** and the **meal inventory** in action (screenshots/animation).
3. Cover the rest of the toolkit (food, workouts, weight trend) as supporting proof of a complete app.
4. Establish trust/values: **no ads, no data-selling, no shame.**
5. Drive the single primary action (download / join waitlist).
